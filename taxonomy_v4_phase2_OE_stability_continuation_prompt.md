# Taxonomy v4.0 — OE Stability & Coaching Quality: Continuation Prompt

## Context

We improved OE (opportunity event) detection stability across the 11-pattern taxonomy, targeting four high-variance patterns: Communication Clarity, Active Listening, Question Quality, and Recognition. OE count instability is the upstream cause of pattern score instability. We ran a fresh 10x10 eval baseline (Phase Q), diagnosed root causes, implemented targeted taxonomy fixes, tested iteratively on 3 transcripts, and ran a new 10x10 eval (Phase R) to establish a post-fix baseline. The next priority is coaching quality improvements in Stage 2.

IMPORTANT NOTE FOR THE SESSION: Never make assumptions. If anything is ambiguous, inconsistent, underspecified, or otherwise unclear, always stop and ask questions before proceeding. Precision and correctness are 10x more important than development speed.

### The 11 patterns (fixed output order)

**Task Effectiveness:** purposeful_framing, focus_management, resolution_and_alignment, assignment_clarity, question_quality, communication_clarity

**Relational Effectiveness:** active_listening, recognition, behavioral_integrity, disagreement_navigation, feedback_quality

### Three goals (in priority order)

1. **Maximize coaching quality** — the primary source of user value. Coaching is generated by Stage 2, which operates independently from Stage 1 scoring. The coaching prompt's calibration (when to give substantive coaching vs. status cards) is the main lever.
2. **Optimize pattern scoring stability** — intra-meeting (same transcript, different runs should produce similar scores) and discriminant validity (different transcripts should produce meaningfully different scores). Scores serve a secondary value: quantification, progress tracking over time, and future cross-leader/team comparison.
3. **Minimize OE detection variability** — strongly correlated with goal #2. OE counts are the denominators in pattern score calculations; unstable counts drive unstable scores.

### Stage architecture

- **Stage 1** (scoring): System prompt is `system_prompt_scoring_v1.0.txt`. Developer message is the **full taxonomy** (`clearvoice_pattern_taxonomy_v4.0.txt`). Generates scores, OEs, evidence spans. Does NOT generate coaching fields.
- **Stage 2** (coaching): System prompt is `system_prompt_coaching_v1.0.txt` with `__PATTERN_DEFINITIONS__` replaced by a condensed extraction from the taxonomy. Stage 2 does NOT see GENERAL_DETECTION_GUIDANCE, full detection notes, or taxonomy coaching instructions. See `extract_stage2_pattern_definitions()` in `backend/core/prompt_builder.py`.
- **Baseline pack**: System prompt is `system_prompt_baseline_pack_v0_6_0.txt`. Generates both scoring and coaching in one pass.

This means: taxonomy changes affect Stage 1 scoring (and indirectly Stage 2 via better scores). Coaching prompt changes directly affect Stage 2 output.

### Key terms

- **OE**: Opportunity Event. A specific moment in the transcript that qualifies for scoring under a pattern. The number of OEs is the pattern's denominator (opportunity_count).
- **OE count range**: max - min OE count across N runs of the same transcript. Lower = more stable.
- **IQR**: Interquartile range of OE counts. More robust than range because it ignores outlier runs.
- **SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio)**: Cross-transcript IQR / mean intra-transcript IQR. Higher = better discrimination between meetings.
- **Range as % of mean**: OE count range divided by mean count. Normalizes across patterns with different count magnitudes.

### OE Stability Improvements (taxonomy changes)

We implemented two rounds of taxonomy changes targeting the four highest-variance patterns (CC, QQ, AL, Recognition), tested iteratively on 3 transcripts x 10 runs.

#### Iteration 1: OE consistency gate filter questions (commit `6266fe1`)

Added a self-test question to the Detection notes section of each pattern. The filter forces the LLM to evaluate whether a moment genuinely qualifies as an opportunity before counting it. Each gate includes "when in doubt, exclude — consistency matters more than completeness."

**Active Listening:** "Is this a moment where the other participant's sense of being heard or ignored would be meaningfully shaped by how the target speaker responds? Does the prior contribution carry enough substance that the target speaker's engagement with it — or failure to engage — matters to the contributor and the conversation?"

**Recognition:** "Is this a moment where the target speaker's choice to show — or withhold — regard for another person as a valued equal carries real interpersonal weight? Would a thoughtful observer notice the presence or absence of recognition here?" Includes explicit list of all recognition behaviors to prevent scope narrowing.

**Communication Clarity:** "Is there something genuinely assessable about how this person communicated — either notably clear or notably unclear? Would a communication coach watching this moment have something specific to say about the speaker's communication clarity?" This was a semantic change — CC's denominator was previously any 40+ word turn; the filter adds a noteworthiness requirement.

**Question Quality:** "Is the primary purpose of this utterance to seek information, test assumptions, surface perspectives, or deepen understanding? Or is the question form incidental — the speaker is really directing, redirecting, asserting, or managing, and the question syntax is just packaging?" Essentially reinforces the existing functional classification rule as a required pre-check.

**Iter1 results (absolute OE count range, 3 transcripts):**
- CC: Improved on all 3 transcripts (8→4, 3→2, 5→3)
- QQ: Improved on M-000001 (13→8) and M-000004 (3→1); minor regression on M-000002 (0→2)
- Recognition: Improved on M-000001 (6→3) and M-000004 (4→3); regressed on M-000002 (3→5)
- AL: Improved on M-000001 (16→9); **regressed** on M-000002 (14→18) and M-000004 (4→6)

#### Diagnosis: Why AL resisted the filter question

Deep analysis of M-000002 AL OEs revealed:
- 41 unique OE turn ranges across 10 runs, but only 1 appeared in 9+ runs
- Clustering overlapping turn boundaries reduced 41 to 25 distinct conversational moments
- The stable core (6 moments in 8+ runs) were clear-cut listening failures; the ephemeral moments (19 in 1-2 runs) were borderline moments that primarily belonged to other patterns (DN, R&A, FM)
- AL scores were similar despite wildly different OE counts (0.333 with 6 OEs vs 0.458 with 24 OEs)
- Coaching output was identical across low and high OE-count runs — same diagnosis, same examples, same themes
- Root cause: in a meeting with pervasive listening failure, the filter question "would the contributor's sense of being heard be meaningfully shaped?" is true for almost every turn — it can't discriminate

#### Iteration 2: AL primary-function gate + meeting-level context (commit `7ec40a2`)

Two changes:

1. **Pattern Priority Rules preamble** (general, applies to all patterns): "First, consider the meeting's dominant coaching themes. Where a single utterance could be classified under more than one pattern, use the dominant coaching themes to inform which pattern captures the most coaching-relevant dimension of the moment as you apply the following rules."

2. **AL primary-function gate** (new entry in Pattern Priority Rules, before the AL multi-pattern rules): "When an active_listening moment co-occurs with another pattern (DN, R&A, FM, BI), ask: is the listening dimension the primary coaching story of this moment, or is another pattern's dimension more salient? Count under AL when the most coaching-relevant observation is about absorption of prior input. If the primary story is conflict handling (DN), decision closure (R&A), topic management (FM), or integrity (BI), and AL would merely echo the same failure from a listening angle, classify under the other pattern only — unless AL captures a distinct absorption behavior that the other pattern's score does not reflect."

Also added cross-reference in AL's "Excluded from denominator" section pointing to the gate.

Modeled on the existing BI repackaging check precedent, but uses a primary-function test (like FM vs DN) rather than a repackaging assumption, because AL is often the upstream cause of other pattern failures (poor listening → premature decisions, poor conflict handling, etc.) and should not be systematically deprioritized.

### Priority 1: Coaching quality improvements (Stage 2)

The Phase Q judge data identified three high-pedantic patterns that need Stage 2 coaching prompt work:

**Recognition: 36.2% pedantic** — worst by far. Pedantic heat map shows 9/10 pedantic on M-000001 (strong facilitator), 6/6 on M-000002, 6/6 on M-000003, 6/9 on M-000006. The system generates recognition coaching on meetings where recognition is irrelevant background competence. This is the same Stage 2 calibration gap identified in the prior coaching quality continuation prompt — the LLM understands the principle but its threshold for "worth a substantive card" is lower than the judge's threshold for "not pedantic."

**Communication Clarity: 17.6% pedantic** — spread across 7 of 10 meetings. CC coaching tends to praise competent-but-unremarkable clarity (4/10 pedantic on M-000001, 3/10 on M-000004). The CC OE noteworthiness filter we added in Stage 1 may help indirectly (fewer unremarkable OEs → less opportunity for pedantic coaching), but the core issue is Stage 2's decision to give CC a substantive card when it shouldn't.

**Behavioral Integrity: 16.1% pedantic** — concentrated in specific meetings (3/7 on M-000003, 3/5 on M-000055, 2/4 on M-000009). BI coaching becomes pedantic when the system praises basic professional integrity as if it were noteworthy.

The coaching prompt's Step 4 (card-mode decisions) is where these calibration issues live. Each pattern gets either a "substantive card" (real coaching content) or a "status card" (brief acknowledgment, no coaching). The LLM's threshold for when a pattern deserves a substantive card is lower than the judge's threshold for "not pedantic" — this is the calibration gap. The prior session's continuation prompt (`taxonomy_v4_phase2_coaching_quality_continuation_prompt_2.md`) has detailed analysis with Stage 2 changelog examples showing the LLM's reasoning.

### Design principles established in this session

These emerged from extended discussion and should guide future taxonomy changes:

1. **Stability over completeness for scoring** — undercounting is better than inconsistent counting. The primary coaching value comes from Stage 2 which sees the full transcript regardless of OE counts. Score stability matters more for the secondary value (tracking, comparison).

2. **Filter questions over mechanical rules** — self-test questions engage the LLM's contextual judgment while focusing it on a specific, consistent criterion. Preferable to numerical caps, turn-count rules, or other mechanistic constraints that risk excluding valid OEs.

3. **Primary-function tests for multi-pattern collisions** — when a moment could belong to multiple patterns, the test should ask "which pattern captures the most coaching-relevant dimension?" without pre-assuming which pattern wins. Modeled on existing FM vs DN precedent.

4. **Meeting-level context informs moment-level classification** — the LLM should consider the meeting's dominant coaching themes when resolving pattern collisions. Now stated in the Pattern Priority Rules preamble.

5. **Examples risk over-anchoring** — providing boundary examples can cause the LLM to match surface features rather than underlying principles. Use sparingly and always pair with the principle (the "why").

6. **OE caps were tried and removed** — hard caps on OE counts (previously existed for CC at 8, AL at 10, Recognition at 10) were removed in commit `cf44a4d` because they created selection bias: dropping OEs could systematically exclude strong or weak examples, skewing scores.

### Approaches considered and rejected

These were discussed at length and deliberately not implemented. Do not re-propose them without revisiting the reasoning:

- **OE count caps** — selection bias risk (which OEs to drop?). In mixed-performance meetings, caps systematically exclude either strong or weak examples, distorting scores.
- **Saturation / stop-counting rules** — temporal bias risk. If the leader improves (or worsens) as the meeting progresses, stop-counting misses the shift.
- **Turn proximity rules for AL** (e.g., "within N turns") — too mechanistic. Meeting pace varies; a hard number can't capture conversational proximity across different meeting styles.
- **Denominator restatement** — risk of over-simplifying the intended definition and backfiring. The compression inherent in a restatement can lose nuance the LLM needs.
- **Pattern-specific "when in doubt, exclude" echoes** — skipped because the general rule already exists in two places (CORE_RULES line 64 and GENERAL_DETECTION_GUIDANCE line 1856). The filter questions are a stronger intervention for the same purpose.
- **Boundary examples without principles** — over-anchoring risk. The LLM may match surface features of examples rather than the underlying principle. If examples are used in the future, always pair with the "why."
- **Two-pass OE detection** (identify candidates, then filter) — too large a structural change to the prompt architecture for uncertain benefit.
