# Prompt: Generate a Coachee Persona for Longitudinal Communication Coaching Test

## Instructions

Create a detailed persona for a leader who will be the subject of a longitudinal communication coaching evaluation. This persona will be used to generate a series of 7-8 realistic meeting transcripts over several weeks, so it needs enough depth and specificity to maintain behavioral consistency across many conversations.

The persona must feel like a real person — someone a coach would recognize after a few sessions. They should have genuine strengths that coexist with meaningful blind spots. Avoid caricatures; the best coaching subjects are competent leaders with specific developmental edges.

## Requirements

### Professional Context
- Name, job title, company type, team size, and reporting structure
- A brief description of their current work situation — what projects or challenges are they dealing with right now? What's the business context? (This gives transcript generators concrete material to build realistic meeting agendas around.)
- What kind of meetings they typically run or participate in (standups, strategy reviews, 1:1s, cross-functional syncs, etc.)
- Their typical role in meetings: Do they usually chair? Sometimes participate without chairing? Present to senior stakeholders?
- 1-2 sentences on their career trajectory and what brought them to this role

### Communication Strengths (describe 2-3)
Describe specific, observable communication behaviors this person does well in meetings. Be concrete:
- NOT "good communicator" or "strong leader"
- YES "consistently opens meetings by naming the 2-3 decisions that need to be made and the time available, which keeps the group focused" or "asks pointed diagnostic questions that cut through surface-level updates to identify the real blocker"

### Communication Weaknesses / Blind Spots (describe 2-3)
Describe specific, observable communication behaviors where this person falls short. These should be realistic blind spots — things a senior coach would notice but the leader might not be fully aware of:
- NOT "bad at communication" or "needs to improve"
- YES "tends to close discussions and move to the next topic before confirming that everyone is aligned on what was just decided — people leave the room with different understandings of the outcome" or "when someone pushes back on their proposal, they respond by restating their position more forcefully rather than engaging with the substance of the objection"

### Neutral Habits and Texture
Describe 2-3 communication behaviors that aren't particularly strong or weak — just how this person naturally communicates. These add realism and test whether a coaching system can distinguish signal from noise:
- How do they give routine updates or status reports?
- How do they respond to good news or completed work?
- Do they have any verbal tics, filler phrases, or stylistic tendencies?
- Any habits that are just... fine? Neither coaching gold nor coaching problems?

### Interpersonal Dynamics
Describe how this person's communication varies depending on who they're talking to:
- How do they interact with someone more junior who's still building confidence?
- How do they interact with a peer who tends to push back or challenge them?
- How do they interact with someone they trust deeply versus someone they're still building a relationship with?
- These don't need to be dramatic differences — even subtle shifts are useful

### Meeting Style and Personality
- How do they come across in a room? (e.g., energetic and fast-paced, measured and deliberate, warm but sometimes scattered)
- What is their default behavior under pressure? (e.g., speeds up, becomes more directive, withdraws, gets defensive)

### Key Recurring Colleagues (3-4 people)
Describe the people who appear most often in this leader's meetings. For each:
- Name and role/function
- 1-2 sentences on their personality and communication style in meetings
- Their relationship dynamic with the coachee (e.g., trusted lieutenant, respectful challenger, quiet contributor who has good ideas but rarely speaks up)

At least one recurring colleague must be a **direct report** — someone the coachee manages. This person will appear in 1:1 meetings where the coachee is the manager.

These characters will recur across multiple transcripts, so they need enough definition to remain consistent.

### Highest-Leverage Development Area
In 1-2 sentences, describe the single most impactful behavior change for this person. Describe it in plain language — what would be different if they improved here? Do NOT suggest a specific experiment or coaching intervention; the system should arrive at that independently.

## Output Format

Return the persona as a structured document with clear sections. Use the person's name throughout (not "the leader" or "the coachee"). Write as much as needed to fully capture the persona — enough for a transcript-generating LLM to maintain consistency across 7+ meetings, including the interpersonal dynamics and neutral habits that make transcripts feel real.

## Important

- Do NOT use any technical coaching framework terminology, pattern names, scoring rubrics, or taxonomy language
- Describe everything in plain behavioral language — what the person says and does in meetings, not what category it falls into
- The strengths and weaknesses should naturally co-occur in the same person — they should feel like two sides of the same coin where possible (e.g., someone who is great at driving pace might struggle with giving people space to disagree)
- The persona should support meetings where the coachee chairs, manages a 1:1 with a direct report, or participates in someone else's meeting — include enough context for all three
