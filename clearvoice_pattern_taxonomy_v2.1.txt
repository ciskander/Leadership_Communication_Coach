CLEARVOICE PATTERN TAXONOMY v2.1
Role-conditioned, target-speaker-only, conservative counting.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### BEGIN:CORE_RULES ###

CORE RULES (apply to all patterns)

- Evaluate ONLY the target speaker identified by target_speaker_label.
- Count ONLY target-controlled opportunities. If target control is ambiguous, exclude from both numerator and denominator.
- Apply role-conditioned denominators as specified per pattern below.
- If target-controlled opportunities < minimum threshold, mark insufficient_signal. Never force a ratio.
- Do not infer intent or personality. Score observable verbal behavior only.
- For numeric patterns: denominator = opportunity_count, ratio = numerator/denominator rounded to 4 decimals.
- conversational_balance uses balance_assessment only (no numerator/denominator/ratio).
- Allowed pattern_id only: agenda_clarity, objective_signaling, turn_allocation, facilitative_inclusion, decision_closure, owner_timeframe_specification, summary_checkback, question_quality, listener_response_quality, conversational_balance.
- Always output all 10 patterns in this fixed order.
- Coaching-first output: strengths 0..2, focus exactly 1, micro_experiment exactly 1.
- Focus/micro-experiment selection should prioritize leverageable recurring behavior, with deterministic tie-breakers.
- Missing speaker attribution: attribution-dependent patterns not_evaluable.
- Evidence spans must reference turn-based excerpts with integer turn IDs.

COUNTING DISTINCTION (applies to all patterns)

Each pattern defines two exclusion categories:

- Excluded from numerator only (failed opportunity): The moment counts as an opportunity in the denominator but not as a success in the numerator. This lowers the ratio — it represents a missed chance.
- Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity): The moment is invisible to the pattern entirely. It does not affect the ratio in either direction. Use this for behaviors that fall outside the pattern's scope.

When in doubt, prefer excluding from both. Undercounting is safer than miscounting.

### END:CORE_RULES ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:agenda_clarity ###

PATTERN 1: agenda_clarity

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker opens the meeting (or their segment) with an explicit statement of purpose, scope, and/or desired outcome.

Denominator:
- Meeting or segment openings the target speaker leads or is responsible for.

Counts as opportunity:
- Target speaker has the floor at the start of the meeting or at the start of their agenda segment.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker states what the meeting/segment is for AND what a good outcome looks like. Both elements must be present.

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- A purpose statement without an outcome frame ("We're here to talk about pricing" with no indication of what a good result looks like).
- An outcome frame without a purpose statement ("I want us to leave with a decision" without naming what the decision is about).

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- A greeting or pleasantry without any attempt at framing ("Good morning everyone, thanks for joining").
- Restating the calendar invite title or meeting name without adding purpose or outcome.
- Openings controlled by another speaker (the target speaker is not the one opening).

Role notes:
- Chair: denominator = 1 (the meeting open). Expect explicit purpose + outcome framing.
- Presenter: denominator = 1 (their segment open). Same standard.
- Participant/Report/Manager: mark not_evaluable unless they explicitly open a sub-topic they own.

Minimum threshold:
- 1 opportunity required.

Detection note:
- This pattern is about the opening frame only. Do not score it on mid-meeting behaviors — those belong to objective_signaling.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Stating both what the meeting is for AND what a good outcome looks like.
- Common failure mode: Purpose statement without an outcome frame, or an outcome frame without a purpose statement.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee build the habit of stating both elements — purpose and desired outcome — in their opening.

### END:PATTERN:agenda_clarity ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:objective_signaling ###

PATTERN 2: objective_signaling

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker makes the current topic's purpose explicit when transitioning between topics or sub-topics during the meeting.

Denominator:
- Topic transitions the target speaker initiates or takes ownership of during the meeting body (after the open).

Counts as opportunity:
- Target speaker shifts from one topic to another, or introduces a new agenda item.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker names why the group is shifting AND what the next topic is meant to accomplish. Both elements required. Example: "Let's move to pricing — the goal here is to land a direction, not finalize numbers."

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Naming the next topic without stating its purpose ("OK, let's move to pricing").
- Stating purpose without identifying the topic ("We need to make a decision here" as a transition, without naming what is being transitioned to).

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Transitions initiated by another speaker that the target speaker does not take ownership of.
- The meeting opening (that belongs to agenda_clarity).
- Continuations within the same topic (e.g., "One more thing on this…") — these are not transitions.
- Procedural transitions with no topical content ("Let me share my screen," "Hold on, let me pull that up").

Role notes:
- Chair: high denominator — owns most transitions.
- Presenter: transitions within their segment.
- Participant: only transitions they explicitly initiate.
- 1:1 roles: transitions in the conversation they introduce.

Minimum threshold:
- 2 opportunities required.

Detection note:
- agenda_clarity and objective_signaling are related but distinct. agenda_clarity scores the opening frame once. objective_signaling scores purposeful framing at each subsequent transition. Do not double-count the opening moment.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Naming why the group is shifting AND what the next topic is meant to accomplish.
- Common failure mode: Naming the next topic without stating its purpose ("OK, let's move to pricing").
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee frame transitions with both topic and purpose, not just topic labels.

### END:PATTERN:objective_signaling ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:turn_allocation ###

PATTERN 3: turn_allocation

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker actively distributes speaking turns to others rather than holding the floor disproportionately or allowing one voice to dominate.

Denominator:
- Moments where the target speaker could redirect the floor — after their own speaking turn, after a dominant contributor speaks, or at natural pauses.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker explicitly invites a specific person or role to speak, redirects a dominant voice, or uses a structured go-around. Must be deliberate and verbal.

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- A general open question to the group with no named invitee ("Any thoughts?" / "Questions?").
- A long monologue followed by "any questions?" — the floor-holding itself is the missed opportunity.

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Moments where the turn naturally passes without the target speaker having agency (e.g., a direct question to another participant from a third party).
- Non-verbal acknowledgment (nodding, gestures) — not observable in transcript.
- Moments where only two speakers are present and turn-taking is inherently binary.

Role notes:
- Chair/Facilitator: high denominator — turn management is a core responsibility.
- Presenter: within their segment only.
- Participant: only when they choose to yield and redirect rather than hold the floor.
- 1:1: mark not_evaluable (binary conversation, no turn allocation needed).

Minimum threshold:
- 3 opportunities required (fewer in short meetings — use insufficient_signal).

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Explicitly inviting a specific person or role to speak, redirecting a dominant voice, or using a structured go-around.
- Common failure mode: General open questions with no named invitee ("Any thoughts?"), or long monologues followed by "any questions?"
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee name specific people when inviting contributions, rather than using open-floor prompts.

### END:PATTERN:turn_allocation ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:facilitative_inclusion ###

PATTERN 4: facilitative_inclusion

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker actively brings in quieter voices or underrepresented perspectives, beyond simply distributing turns.

Denominator:
- Moments where ≥3 participants are present AND a quieter/not-yet-heard participant has relevant standing on the topic AND the target speaker has the opportunity to invite them.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker names a person who hasn't spoken on this topic and explicitly invites their view.
- Or explicitly builds on a quieter participant's earlier point to amplify it.
- Or asks for a perspective that has been absent from the discussion.

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Acknowledging a quiet participant's presence without inviting their input ("I know Sarah's here too" without asking for her view).
- Inviting input from someone who has already spoken extensively on this topic — that is turn redistribution, not inclusion of an unheard voice.

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Open group questions ("What does everyone think?") — no specific quieter voice is being brought in.
- Redirecting a dominant speaker without bringing in a specific quieter voice (that is turn_allocation).
- Moments where all participants have already contributed substantively to the current topic.
- Meetings with fewer than 3 participants (no "quieter voice" dynamic possible).

Role notes:
- Chair: full meeting denominator.
- Presenter: within their segment.
- Participant: only deliberate inclusion acts they initiate, not expected facilitation behaviors.
- 1:1 meetings: mark not_evaluable.

Minimum threshold:
- 2 opportunities required.

Detection note:
- This pattern requires noticing who has NOT spoken. If the transcript doesn't give enough information to identify underrepresented voices, mark insufficient_signal.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Naming a person who hasn't spoken on a topic and explicitly inviting their view, or amplifying a quieter participant's earlier point.
- Common failure mode: Acknowledging a quiet participant's presence without inviting their input, or relying on open group questions.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee notice who hasn't spoken and explicitly invite their perspective by name.

### END:PATTERN:facilitative_inclusion ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:decision_closure ###

PATTERN 5: decision_closure

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker brings decision-ready conversations to a clear, named conclusion rather than leaving them open or ambiguous.

Denominator:
- Decision moments the target speaker controls — meaning they introduced the proposal, were asked to decide, or took explicit ownership of closing the question.

Counts as success (strong closure):
- Target speaker states the decision in concrete terms, names what was decided and on what basis, and signals that the question is closed. Example: "We're going with option A — Jen owns it, decision is locked."

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Vague summaries that gesture at alignment without naming the decision ("So we're roughly aligned here, let's keep going").
- Deferral without a concrete revisit date or trigger ("Let's revisit this later" / "We'll come back to it").
- Stating a preference without closing ("I think we should go with A" without confirming the group is aligned and the question is resolved).

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Decisions closed by another speaker that the target speaker did not introduce, shape, or own.
- Informational items or status updates with no decision at stake.
- Exploratory discussions that are not yet decision-ready (the group is still diverging, not converging).
- Decisions that were pre-made and merely announced — no closure behavior is needed.

Role notes:
- Chair: all decision moments in the meeting.
- Presenter: decisions within their topic area.
- Participant: only decisions they introduced, were asked to close, or actively shaped toward closure.
- 1:1 Manager: all decisions discussed.
- 1:1 Report: decisions they propose or are responsible for.

Minimum threshold:
- 2 target-controlled decision moments required.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Stating the decision in concrete terms, naming what was decided, and signaling that the question is closed.
- Common failure mode: Vague summaries that gesture at alignment ("So we're roughly aligned here"), or deferral without a concrete revisit date.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee explicitly name the decision and confirm it is closed before moving on.

### END:PATTERN:decision_closure ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:owner_timeframe_specification ###

PATTERN 6: owner_timeframe_specification

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker assigns action items with both a named owner AND a concrete deadline.

Denominator:
- Action items the target speaker initiates, negotiates, or explicitly accepts responsibility for.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker states who owns the action (a specific name, not "someone" or "the team") AND states when it is due (a specific date or day, not "soon" or "end of quarter").

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Owner is named but no deadline is given ("James, can you handle that?").
- Deadline is given but no owner is named ("We need this by Friday" without specifying who).
- Both owner and deadline are vague ("Someone should get to this soon").

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Action items assigned by others that the target speaker does not participate in specifying, negotiating, or accepting.
- Vague references to future work with no concrete task attached ("We should think about this at some point").
- Status updates on previously assigned actions (re-reporting, not new assignment).
- Actions that are already complete or in progress and merely being acknowledged.

Role notes:
- Chair: all assignments in the meeting.
- Presenter: assignments within their initiative.
- Participant: assignments they initiate, negotiate, or accept.
- 1:1 Manager: all task assignments discussed.
- 1:1 Report: assignments they accept or clarify.

Minimum threshold:
- 2 target-controlled assignment moments required.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Stating who owns the action (a specific name) AND when it is due (a specific date or day).
- Common failure mode: Owner named but no deadline, or deadline given but no owner named.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee pair every action item with both a name and a date.

### END:PATTERN:owner_timeframe_specification ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:summary_checkback ###

PATTERN 7: summary_checkback

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker recaps key points and checks for alignment before closing a topic or transitioning.

Denominator:
- Topic closure moments and major transition points the target speaker owns.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker restates what was decided or agreed, AND either explicitly checks for alignment ("Does that capture it?") or pauses for confirmation before moving on. Both elements required.

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Recap without a check ("So we said we'd go with option B. Moving on…" — restates but does not invite confirmation).
- Check without a recap ("Everyone good? OK, next topic" — invites confirmation but does not restate what was agreed).
- Transition with neither element.

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Transitions or topic closures controlled by another speaker.
- Minor sub-point shifts within the same topic (not a major transition).
- Someone else summarizing while the target speaker moves on — the target speaker did not own the moment.
- End-of-meeting sign-offs with no substantive topic to recap ("Thanks everyone, talk next week").

Role notes:
- Chair: all transition opportunities.
- Presenter: within their segment.
- Participant: only when they attempt to recap or restate — do not penalize for transitions they don't control.
- 1:1 Manager/Report: transitions in the conversation.

Minimum threshold:
- 2 opportunities required.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Restating what was decided or agreed AND checking for confirmation ("Does that capture it?").
- Common failure mode: Recap without a check, or check without a recap, or transition with neither.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee build the two-part habit: restate, then check.

### END:PATTERN:summary_checkback ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:question_quality ###

PATTERN 8: question_quality

What it measures:
- Whether the questions the target speaker asks are purposeful and decision-linked, rather than open-ended explorations without a clear aim.

Denominator:
- Substantive questions the target speaker asks of others during the meeting.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Question is tied to a specific decision, gap, or action the group needs to resolve. The question's purpose is evident from context. Example: "Thomas, if we use 'intelligent recommendations,' does that require a disclaimer on the homepage?"

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Open exploratory questions with no clear decision stake ("What do you all think about our approach?").
- Multi-part questions that invite broad discussion rather than unlocking a specific answer.
- Leading questions that assert a position in question form ("Don't you think we should just go with option A?").

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Rhetorical questions not expecting an answer ("Who wouldn't want that?").
- Clarifying questions that merely restate or echo what was said ("So you're saying it's delayed?").
- Procedural, logistical, or technical questions about meeting mechanics — e.g., audio/video checks ("Can you hear me?", "Is my screen sharing?"), roll call, quorum checks, scheduling logistics ("What time do we need to wrap?"). These are meeting housekeeping, not leadership communication.
- Questions directed at the target speaker by others (wrong direction — the target speaker is receiving, not asking).
- Self-directed thinking-aloud phrased as questions ("What was I going to say?").

Role notes:
- Apply consistently across all roles. Role affects volume of questions, not the standard.

Minimum threshold:
- 3 substantive questions asked by the target speaker required.

Detection note:
- Short, precise questions that unlock a specific answer score higher than long multi-part questions that invite broad discussion.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Questions tied to a specific decision, gap, or action the group needs to resolve.
- Common failure mode: Open exploratory questions with no decision stake, or multi-part questions that invite broad discussion.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee ask shorter, sharper questions that target a specific decision or action.

### END:PATTERN:question_quality ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:listener_response_quality ###

PATTERN 9: listener_response_quality

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker acknowledges and builds on what others have said before responding, rather than pivoting immediately to their own position.

Denominator:
- Moments where the target speaker responds after another participant has made a substantive point directed at or relevant to them.

Counts as success (numerator):
- Target speaker explicitly references or paraphrases what was just said before adding their view. Example: "That's the constraint Naomi raised — if we go above sixty-five, onboarding quality drops. So the question is…"

Excluded from numerator only (counts as failed opportunity):
- Pivoting immediately to a counter-argument or new point without acknowledging the prior speaker's contribution.
- Dismissive responses that skip the substance ("Yeah, but here's what I think…" without engaging with what was said).
- Responding to a different point than the one the prior speaker made.

Excluded from both numerator AND denominator (not an opportunity):
- Agreement tokens alone ("right," "yes," "exactly," "mm-hmm") — too brief to evaluate quality of listening in either direction.
- Responses to procedural or logistical statements (no substantive point to acknowledge).
- Moments where the target speaker is responding to a direct factual question (e.g., "What's the budget?" → "Fifty thousand") — no acknowledgment behavior is expected.
- Turns where the target speaker is the first to speak on a new topic (no prior point to acknowledge).
- Responses to one-word or trivial utterances from others.

Role notes:
- Apply consistently across roles.

Minimum threshold:
- 3 response opportunities required.

Reliability note:
- This pattern is harder to score reliably from transcript text alone than other patterns. Short responses may reflect deep listening or none at all. Apply conservative scoring: only score success when the acknowledgment is explicit and verbal. When in doubt, exclude from both numerator and denominator.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: Explicitly referencing or paraphrasing what was just said before adding their view.
- Common failure mode: Pivoting immediately to a counter-argument without acknowledging the prior speaker's contribution.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee build the habit of naming what they heard before stating their own view.

### END:PATTERN:listener_response_quality ###

### BEGIN:PATTERN:conversational_balance ###

PATTERN 10: conversational_balance

What it measures:
- Whether the target speaker's share of speaking time is appropriate given their role and the meeting type. This is a qualitative judgment — no numerator/denominator/ratio.

Output:
- balance_assessment only. Values: over_indexed | balanced | under_indexed | unclear.

Role-conditioned interpretation:
- Chair/Facilitator: moderate-to-high share is appropriate. Flag over_indexed only if the chair is clearly dominating to the point where others cannot contribute.
- Presenter: high share acceptable within their segment. Evaluate the full meeting, not just their segment.
- Participant: evaluate for over-dominance or under-participation relative to the group size and topic relevance.
- 1:1 Manager: expect roughly 40-60% balance; flag if one party holds >75% consistently.
- 1:1 Report: same as above, mirror assessment.

Use unclear when speaker attribution is insufficient to judge.

Denominator rule:
- None. Do not calculate a ratio.

Excluded from assessment entirely:
- Procedural stretches (tech troubleshooting, waiting for latecomers) should not count toward speaking-time share.
- Reading aloud from shared documents or slides (this inflates word count without reflecting conversational dominance).

Detection note:
- If the transcript has incomplete or missing speaker labels, mark unclear.

Experiment guidance:
- What good looks like: A speaking share appropriate to the speaker's role and meeting type. Qualitative — no numeric ratio. Assessment values: over_indexed, balanced, under_indexed, unclear.
- Common failure mode: Over_indexed: Dominating discussion to the point where others cannot contribute. Under_indexed: Not contributing enough given their role and stake in the topics.
- Experiment focus: Help the coachee calibrate their speaking share — either creating more space for others or contributing more actively.

### END:PATTERN:conversational_balance ###

### BEGIN:GENERAL_DETECTION_GUIDANCE ###

GENERAL DETECTION GUIDANCE

Conservative counting in practice:
- "Target-controlled" means the target speaker had clear agency over whether the behavior occurred. If the situation forced a particular response, it may not be a true opportunity — exclude from both numerator and denominator.
- When a behavior is borderline between "failed opportunity" and "not an opportunity," prefer excluding from both. Undercounting is safer than miscounting.

Role-conditioned floor effects:
- Participants have smaller denominators than Chairs by design. A Participant with 2 clean decision closures in a meeting they did not run is demonstrating strong behavior. Do not penalize the role.

Insufficient signal vs. not_evaluable:
- insufficient_signal: the pattern is applicable to this role/meeting type, but there were too few target-controlled opportunities to score it (below minimum threshold).
- not_evaluable: the pattern fundamentally does not apply (e.g., facilitative_inclusion in a 1:1, or a pattern requiring speaker attribution when labels are absent).

### END:GENERAL_DETECTION_GUIDANCE ###
